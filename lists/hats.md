## mens hats 
* ***Top Hat** - Tall, cylindrical formal hat worn for evening dress, weddings, and formal daytime occasions. Essential for gentlemen in Victorian and Edwardian society.
* **Bowler/Derby** - Hard felt hat with rounded crown. Worn by middle-class businessmen and city workers from the 1860s onwards; less formal than a top hat.
* **Boater** - Flat-topped straw hat with ribbon band. Summer wear, associated with boating, picnics, and casual warm-weather occasions from the 1880s-1920s.
* **Deerstalker** - Tweed cap with front and back brims and ear flaps. Country and hunting wear, famously associated with Sherlock Holmes.
* **Flat Cap/Cloth Cap** - Soft, rounded cap with small brim. Working-class men's everyday wear throughout both centuries.
* **Homburg** - Felt hat with center crease and upturned brim. Formal daytime wear, popular from the 1890s.
* **Trilby** - Soft felt hat with narrow brim and indented crown. Less formal alternative to the homburg, popular in the 20th century.

## **women's hats:**

* ***Bonnet** - Close-fitting hat tied under the chin with ribbons. Everyday wear for women throughout the 19th century, particularly respectable married women.
* **Poke Bonnet** - Bonnet with projecting front brim that framed the face. Popular in early-to-mid 19th century.
* **Picture Hat** - Large, wide-brimmed decorated hat. Worn for garden parties and formal daytime occasions in Edwardian era.
* **Cloche** - Close-fitting, bell-shaped hat. Quintessential 1920s fashion, worn pulled down over the forehead.
* **Pillbox** - Small, flat-topped cylindrical hat. Popular from 1930s-1960s for formal daytime wear.

## **clerical hats:**

* **Biretta** - Square cap with three or four ridges on top, sometimes with a pompom. Worn by Roman Catholic priests, particularly during Mass.
* **Shovel Hat** - Low-crowned hat with wide, flat brim turned up at the sides. Worn by Anglican clergy, particularly in the 18th and 19th centuries; somewhat old-fashioned by the late Victorian era.
