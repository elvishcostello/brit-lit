## English Counties Dictionary

**Bedfordshire** - Seen as quiet, middle-England folk. Known for: brick-making, market gardening, and being the birthplace of the Bedfordshire clanger (a pastry).

**Berkshire** - Viewed as well-to-do and conservative, with royal connections. Known for: Royal Windsor, horse racing at Ascot, technology sector around Reading.

**Buckinghamshire** - Stereotyped as affluent commuter-belt residents. Known for: furniture making (especially chairs in High Wycombe), Pinewood Studios, and being very "Home Counties."

**Cambridgeshire** - Seen as intellectual and academic. Known for: Cambridge University, technology/science research, fen farming, and punting.

**Cheshire** - Viewed as prosperous "Cheshire set" - footballers' wives territory. Known for: Cheshire cheese, salt mining, wealthy suburbs of Manchester.

**Cornwall** - Stereotyped as laid-back, pasty-eating surfers with strong regional identity. Known for: pasties, cream teas, tin mining heritage, tourism, fishing, and rugby.

**Cumbria** - Seen as hardy, outdoorsy fell-walkers. Known for: Lake District tourism, sheep farming, Kendal mint cake, pencil making (Derwent).

**Derbyshire** - Viewed as down-to-earth northerners with rural charm. Known for: Peak District, Bakewell pudding, Derby porcelain, limestone quarrying.

**Devon** - Stereotyped as farmers and retirees enjoying cream teas. Known for: cream teas (cream first!), cider, farming, tourism, and having a strong rural identity.

**Dorset** - Seen as genteel, rural, and traditional. Known for: Jurassic Coast fossils, Thomas Hardy connections, Blue Vinny cheese, and being very "olde worlde."

**Durham** - Viewed as proud mining communities with strong traditions. Known for: coal mining heritage, Durham Cathedral, the Prince Bishops' history.

**Essex** - Stereotyped (often unfairly) as flashy, fake-tanned, and materialistic - "Essex girls/boys." Known for: being commuter territory, market towns, and proximity to London.

**Gloucestershire** - Seen as rural, well-spoken West Country folk. Known for: Cotswolds tourism, Double Gloucester cheese, cheese rolling, cider.

**Hampshire** - Viewed as naval, military, and relatively prosperous. Known for: Royal Navy (Portsmouth), New Forest, watercress, Jane Austen.

**Herefordshire** - Stereotyped as traditional farmers and cider makers. Known for: Hereford cattle, cider and perry production, hops, SAS headquarters.

**Hertfordshire** - Seen as affluent commuter-belt families. Known for: being dormitory towns for London, film studios, new towns like Welwyn Garden City.

**Kent** - Called "The Garden of England" - seen as prosperous and traditional. Known for: fruit growing (especially apples and cherries), hops, being the gateway to Europe.

**Lancashire** - Stereotyped as friendly, working-class northerners with strong accents. Known for: cotton mills heritage, Lancashire hotpot, Blackpool, football rivalries.

**Leicestershire** - Viewed as solid, working-class Midlanders. Known for: hosiery, Stilton cheese, pork pies (Melton Mowbray), Leicester City FC.

**Lincolnshire** - Seen as agricultural "yellowbellies" (affectionate local term). Known for: farming (especially vegetables), sausages, bomber airfields, flat landscapes.

**Norfolk** - Stereotyped as rural, insular "Norfolk dumplings" with distinctive accent. Known for: farming, Broads tourism, turkeys, fishing, and mustard (Colman's).

**Northamptonshire** - Viewed as quiet Midlanders, often overlooked. Known for: shoemaking, Silverstone racing, being the "Rose of the Shires."

**Northumberland** - Seen as hardy, independent border people. Known for: castles, Hadrian's Wall, wild landscapes, Northumberland sausages.

**Nottinghamshire** - Stereotyped as Robin Hood folk, mining communities. Known for: coal mining heritage, Nottingham lace, Sherwood Forest, Byron.

**Oxfordshire** - Viewed as privileged, academic, "posh." Known for: Oxford University, publishing, the Cotswolds edge, morris dancing traditions.

**Rutland** - Seen as quaint, rural, independent-minded (England's smallest county). Known for: Rutland Water, rural tourism, keeping their identity.

**Shropshire** - Stereotyped as quiet rural farmers, traditional. Known for: agriculture, Ironbridge (birthplace of Industrial Revolution), Shropshire Blue cheese.

**Somerset** - Viewed as cider-drinking farmers with rural charm. Known for: cider, Cheddar cheese, Glastonbury Festival, levels and wetlands.

**Staffordshire** - Seen as working-class pottery folk. Known for: The Potteries (Stoke-on-Trent), ceramics, oatcakes, Staffordshire bull terriers.

**Suffolk** - Stereotyped as agricultural "Suffolk punches" (from the horse breed). Known for: farming, fishing (Lowestoft), Adnams brewery, rural traditions.

**Surrey** - Viewed as wealthy, conservative stockbroker belt. Known for: being expensive, commuter territory, not much actual production but lots of money.

**Sussex** (East and West) - Seen as either trendy (Brighton) or genteel retirees. Known for: seaside resorts, South Downs, Arundel Castle, wine production.

**Warwickshire** - Viewed as prosperous Midlanders with cultural heritage. Known for: Shakespeare (Stratford), Warwick Castle, market gardening.

**West Midlands** - Stereotyped as Brummie industrialists with distinctive accent. Known for: manufacturing, cars (historic), Cadbury chocolate, curry (Balti Triangle).

**Wiltshire** - Seen as rural, mysterious (Stonehenge), military. Known for: Stonehenge, military bases (Salisbury Plain), pigs and bacon.

**Worcestershire** - Viewed as rural fruit growers and sauce makers. Known for: Worcestershire sauce, fruit growing (Vale of Evesham), Royal Worcester porcelain.

**Yorkshire** - Stereotyped as proud, blunt, thrifty "Tykes" who love cricket and tea. Known for: wool and textiles heritage, Yorkshire pudding, cricket, brewing, coal mining, "God's Own County" pride.

## Scottish Council Areas/Regions Dictionary

**Aberdeenshire/Aberdeen City** - Stereotyped as dour, thrifty "Teuchters" (rural folk) or oil-rich. Known for: North Sea oil and gas, granite (the "Granite City"), fishing, Aberdeen Angus cattle.

**Angus** - Seen as agricultural and down-to-earth. Known for: Arbroath smokies (smoked haddock), farming, soft fruit, and Angus beef.

**Argyll and Bute** - Viewed as remote Highlanders and islanders. Known for: whisky distilling, seafood, tourism, stunning scenery, and Gaelic culture.

**Ayrshire** - Stereotyped as Burns Country folk, agricultural. Known for: Robert Burns heritage, Ayrshire potatoes, dairy farming, Ayrshire bacon.

**Borders (Scottish)** - Seen as independent-minded, rugby-loving. Known for: wool and textile mills, Common Ridings, rugby, and the ballad tradition.

**Dumfries and Galloway** - Viewed as rural, agricultural southerners. Known for: farming, Robert Burns connections, dairy, and being Scotland's "forgotten corner."

**Dundee** - Stereotyped as working-class with a strong accent. Known for: the "three Js" (jute, jam, and journalism), video games industry, Dundee cake.

**East Lothian** - Seen as prosperous, golf-playing commuters. Known for: golf courses, farming, beaches, proximity to Edinburgh.

**Edinburgh/Midlothian** - Viewed as posh, cultured, "fur coat and nae knickers." Known for: financial services, festivals (Fringe, Hogmanay), tourism, whisky, shortbread.

**Falkirk** - Stereotyped as industrial, working-class. Known for: the Kelpies, Falkirk Wheel, industrial heritage, proximity to central belt.

**Fife** - Called "the Kingdom" - seen as proud, distinctive, working-class. Known for: coal mining heritage, fishing villages, golf (St Andrews), and saying they're from "the Kingdom."

**Glasgow/Greater Glasgow** - Stereotyped as tough, friendly "hardmen" with sharp wit. Known for: shipbuilding heritage, "Glasgow kiss," engineering, deep-fried Mars bars, and being Scotland's largest city.

**Highlands** - Viewed as romantic Gaelic-speaking crofters. Known for: whisky, tourism, crofting, Highland cattle, Loch Ness, and spectacular scenery.

**Inverclyde** - Seen as shipbuilding communities, working-class. Known for: shipbuilding heritage (Greenock), sugar refining, maritime history.

**Lanarkshire** - Stereotyped as mining and steel communities. Known for: coal and steel heritage, New Lanark mills, industrial past.

**Moray** - Viewed as whisky makers and fishing folk. Known for: Speyside whisky trail (half of Scotland's distilleries), fishing, RAF bases.

**Orkney** - Seen as Norse-influenced, independent islanders. Known for: renewable energy, Viking heritage, farming, fishing, archaeological sites, and insisting they're not Scottish but Orcadian.

**Perth and Kinross** - Viewed as rural, prosperous "Perthshire" folk. Known for: soft fruit (raspberries), whisky, tourism, "the Fair City" (Perth).

**Renfrewshire** - Stereotyped as industrial, working-class. Known for: Paisley pattern, thread making (Coats), Glasgow Airport.

**Shetland** - Seen as fiercely independent Vikings, not really Scottish. Known for: oil terminals (Sullom Voe), fishing, Fair Isle knitwear, Up Helly Aa fire festival, ponies.

**Stirling** - Viewed as historically-minded, central. Known for: tourism (Stirling Castle, Wallace Monument), being Scotland's historic heart.

**Western Isles (Outer Hebrides)** - Stereotyped as Gaelic-speaking crofters, religious. Known for: Harris Tweed, crofting, Gaelic language stronghold, Sabbath observance, peat cutting.

## Welsh Counties Dictionary

**Anglesey (Ynys MÃ´n)** - Seen as island farmers, Welsh-speaking. Known for: farming, being "Mam Cymru" (Mother of Wales) for grain, tourism, Menai bridges.

**Blaenau Gwent** - Stereotyped as Valleys folk, mining communities. Known for: coal mining heritage, male voice choirs, strong working-class identity.

**Bridgend** - Viewed as industrial south Walians. Known for: manufacturing, coal heritage, being between Cardiff and Swansea.

**Caerphilly** - Stereotyped as Valleys miners with strong accents. Known for: Caerphilly cheese, coal mining heritage, castles.

**Cardiff/Vale of Glamorgan** - Seen as cosmopolitan, "not really Welsh" by some. Known for: capital city, rugby (Principality Stadium), coal export heritage, media, government.

**Carmarthenshire** - Viewed as rural, Welsh-speaking farmers. Known for: farming, Welsh language, Dylan Thomas connections, being very traditionally Welsh.

**Ceredigion** - Stereotyped as "Cardis" - thrifty, Welsh-speaking sheep farmers. Known for: sheep farming, Welsh language stronghold, Aberystwyth University, being very rural.

**Conwy** - Seen as tourist-oriented, castle country. Known for: castles (Conwy, Caernarfon), slate quarrying, tourism, coastal resorts.

**Denbighshire** - Viewed as rural north Walians, agricultural. Known for: farming, tourism, Welsh language areas, market towns.

**Flintshire** - Stereotyped as anglicized, not "properly Welsh." Known for: manufacturing, proximity to England, having a foot in both camps.

**Gwynedd** - Seen as the "most Welsh" - Gaelic heartland, nationalist. Known for: Snowdonia, slate (Blaenau Ffestiniog), Welsh language bastion, Plaid Cymru stronghold.

**Merthyr Tydfil** - Stereotyped as proud Valleys folk, industrial heritage. Known for: iron and steel heritage, being the heart of industrial Wales, strong community identity.

**Monmouthshire** - Viewed as ambiguously Welsh/English, posh borders. Known for: farming, historically disputed whether in Wales or England, prosperous.

**Neath Port Talbot** - Seen as steel workers, industrial. Known for: steel works (Port Talbot), coal heritage, rugby, strong industrial identity.

**Newport** - Stereotyped as industrial, working-class, overlooked. Known for: steel, docks, being between Cardiff and Bristol, Chartist uprising.

**Pembrokeshire** - Called "Little England Beyond Wales" - seen as less Welsh. Known for: tourism, stunning coastline, oil refineries (Milford Haven), English-speaking majority in south.

**Powys** - Viewed as sparse, agricultural, sheep country. Known for: sheep farming, being rural and sparsely populated, the "desert of Wales," market towns.

**Rhondda Cynon Taf** - Stereotyped as the archetypal Valleys - miners, choirs, rugby. Known for: coal mining heritage, male voice choirs, rugby, strong community bonds, terraced houses.

**Swansea** - Viewed as industrial, rugby-mad, Dylan Thomas folk. Known for: copper smelting ("Copperopolis"), Dylan Thomas, rugby, being Wales' second city, "Swansea Jacks."

**Torfaen** - Seen as Valleys communities, industrial. Known for: coal and steel heritage, being the eastern valleys.

**Wrexham** - Stereotyped as "not properly Welsh," anglicized. Known for: brewing (Wrexham Lager), coal, proximity to England, football rather than rugby.

There's your complete British counties dictionary!

## Fictional counties
Excellent question! Here are some notable fictional British counties:

**Barsetshire** - Anthony Trollope's creation (1855 onwards), later revived by Angela Thirkell. West Country location, known for its cathedral city and clerical politics.

**Borsetshire** - From BBC Radio's "The Archers" (1950-present). The longest-running soap opera's setting, based loosely on the West Midlands, centered on the village of Ambridge.

**Rutshire** - Jilly Cooper's "Rutshire Chronicles" (1985 onwards). Home to wealthy, scandalous aristocrats, show jumpers, and politicians behaving badly. Very "Home Counties."

**Midsomer** - From ITV's "Midsomer Murders" (1997-present). The deadliest county in Britain, with impossibly high murder rates in picture-perfect villages.

**Wessex** - Thomas Hardy's semi-fictional county (based on Dorset and surrounding areas). Encompasses several renamed real locations for his tragic rural novels.

**Trumptonshire** - From 1960s children's TV (Camberwick Green, Trumpton, Chigley). Idyllic, innocent stop-motion world.

**Letherbridge** - From BBC's "Doctors" soap opera. Modern-day medical drama setting.

**Grantshire** - Various crime novels and TV shows have used this generic Midlands county.

**Middlemarch** - George Eliot's fictional Midlands town, sometimes treated as giving its name to the county.

**Denton** - Not a county but the fictional town from "A Touch of Frost," worth mentioning as a quintessentially grim British setting.
