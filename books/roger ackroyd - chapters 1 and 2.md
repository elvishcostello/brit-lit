Here are the distinctively British terms from these Agatha Christie chapters:

## **Medical/Professional Terms**

- **surgery** - doctor's office/consultation room (Americans: "doctor's office")
- **surgery patients** - patients seen at the doctor's office
- **on my round** - making house calls to patients
- **been sent for** - been called/summoned (to a patient)

## **Household Staff & Roles**

- **parlormaid** - female servant who serves at table
- **house parlormaid** - specific type of maid
- **under housemaid** - junior housemaid
- **kitchenmaid** - kitchen servant
- **housekeeper** - senior female servant managing household
- **housekeeper's room** - housekeeper's private sitting room

## **Social/Class Terms**

- **country squire** - landed gentleman/rural estate owner
- **the vicar** - Anglican parish priest
- **parish funds** - church community funds
- **village green** - common grassy area in village center
- **high society** - upper class society

## **Places & Geography**

- **King's Abbot** - village name (typical British format)
- **Cranchester** - nearby town
- **railway station** - train station
- **General Stores** - village shops
- **the big town** - regional center

## **Idioms & Expressions**

- **just over a year ago** - slightly more than a year
- **coming on** - developing/approaching ("been coming on for six months")
- **on the tapis** - under discussion/consideration (from French, but used in British English)
- **get hold of** - find/catch (someone)
- **look here** - attention-getting phrase
- **damn!** - exclamation (more shocking in British context of that era)
- **the worst of it was** - the unfortunate part
- **no smoke without fire** - where there's smoke, there's fire
- **nothing wrong about him** - nothing bad/suspicious
- **thirsting for information** - eager for gossip
- **gone about** - circulated/spread around

## **Substances & Medications**

- **veronal** - barbiturate sleeping medication (brand name more common in UK)
- **liniment** - topical pain relief medication
- **sal volatile** - smelling salts/aromatic spirits of ammonia
- **curare** - South American arrow poison (mentioned in medical context)
- **cocaine habit** - cocaine addiction (discussed as medical issue)

## **Food & Dining**

- **eggs and bacon** - typical British breakfast
- **luncheon gong** - gong signaling lunch is served (very upper-class British)

## **Titles & Descriptions**

- **dipsomaniac** - alcoholic (clinical/formal term, more British usage)
- **rubicund** - red-faced, ruddy
- **redoubtable** - formidable/impressive

## **Money & Property**

- **well off** - wealthy/affluent
- **mean in personal expenditure** - stingy with personal spending
- **the bread of charity** - depending on others' charity
- **extremely mean** - very stingy

## **British Institutions**

- **Lads' Clubs** - youth organizations
- **Disabled Soldiers' Institutes** - veterans' support organizations
- **cricket matches** - cricket games (quintessentially British)

## **Other Distinctly British Phrases**

- **early in life** - at a young age
- **summed up in the one word** - summarized as
- **hand and glove with** - close friends with
- **subscribes liberally to** - donates generously to
- **the life and soul of** - the central/most important person in
- **we are all very fond of** - we all like
- **very well together** - got along well
- **came to live here** - moved here
- **proper place** - appropriate position/status
- **trump up** - fabricate/invent falsely
- **in duty bound** - obligated by duty
- **stepped out** - emerged from

## **Time Expressions**

- **at eight o'clock on the morning** - (British phrasing vs. "at 8 AM")
- **just over a year ago** - slightly more than a year
- **at 7.30** - 7:30 (British uses full stop, not colon)

## **Characteristically British Narrative Style**

- **to tell the truth** - honestly speaking
- **as I say** - as I mentioned
- **I don't know how she manages it, but there it is** - characteristic British understatement
- **one always has to** - one must (formal "one" instead of "you")

## **Notable Observations**

This passage has fewer aristocratic titles than the Sayers text, but shows more **village life terminology** and **medical practice terms**. The class distinctions are still very present but expressed through household staff hierarchy rather than peerage. The narrative voice itself is distinctly British middle-class professional.
