Absolutely! Let me break this down with literary context:

**Idioms & their origins (crucial for period fiction):**

- "Cut to the quick" - wounded deeply (quick = living flesh)
- "On the shelf" - unmarried woman past prime marriageable age
- "In one's black books" - in disfavor
- "Beyond the pale" - outside acceptable society
- "Fast" (as adjective for women) - morally questionable
- "Make a good fist of it" - do reasonably well
- "At sixes and sevens" - in confusion/disagreement
- "Come to grief" - fail or have an accident
- "Cut one dead" - deliberately ignore socially
- "Give the cut direct" - most severe social snub
- "Make a mull of" - botch something
- "In queer street" - in financial trouble
- "Pluck" (noun) - courage, spirit
- "Gumption" - initiative, common sense
- "Up a gum tree" - in difficulty

**Upper-class understatement (Wodehouse, Sayers, etc.):**

- "Bit thick" - outrageous, unfair
- "Rum do" - strange situation
- "Off one's chump" - crazy
- "Dashed awkward" - extremely problematic
- "Rather!" - emphatic yes
- "I say!" - expression of surprise
- "Steady on!" - calm down
- "That's torn it" - that's ruined everything
- "In a stew" - anxious, worried
- "Blue funk" - state of fear
- "Bally" / "blasted" / "blinking" - mild expletives
- "Rum customer" - odd person
- "Wash out" - failure, disappointment

**Victorian/Edwardian expressions:**

- "Fast asleep" - deeply asleep (different from "fast woman"!)
- "Cutting a dash" - making an impression
- "Kicked up a dust" - caused a scandal
- "Put about" - upset, disturbed
- "Bowled over" - amazed/smitten
- "All to pieces" - emotionally distraught
- "In a taking" - agitated state
- "Making a dead set" - pursuing romantically/aggressively
- "Particular friends" - engaged or as good as
- "An understanding" - informal engagement
- "Get one's own back" - revenge

**1920s-30s slang (Bright Young Things era):**

- "Topping" / "ripping" / "spiffing" - excellent
- "Wizard" / "wizard wheeze" - great idea
- "Priceless" - hilarious
- "Too utter" / "utterly" - extreme
- "Rot" - nonsense
- "Piffle" - foolish talk
- "Tosh" - rubbish
- "Balderdash" - nonsense
- "Bunkum" - nonsense (from Buncombe)
- "Codswallop" - nonsense
- "Flummoxed" - confused
- "Befuddled" - confused
- "Bamboozled" - deceived
- "Barmy" - crazy
- "Potty" - crazy
- "Round the bend" - crazy

**False friends (critical for American readers!):**

- **Nervy** - brave (NOT nervous)
- **Homely** - homelike, comfortable (NOT unattractive)
- **Quite** - somewhat OR completely (context dependent!)
- **Momentarily** - for a moment (NOT "in a moment")
- **Presently** - soon (NOT currently)
- **Directly** - soon (NOT immediately)
- **Mad** - angry, not insane (usually)
- **Knackered** - exhausted (NOT injured)
- **Peckish** - slightly hungry
- **Pissed** - drunk (NOT angry)
- **Fag** - cigarette OR tedious task OR junior boy serving senior
- **Rubber** - eraser (NOT condom in this context!)
- **Slash** - urinate
- **Sleeping with** - sharing a room (in older texts, not sexual)
- **Intercourse** - conversation (in Victorian texts!)

**Bodily function euphemisms:**

- "Spend a penny" - use toilet (from pay toilets)
- "Powder one's nose" - ladies' room
- "Wash one's hands" - use toilet
- "Pay a call" - use toilet
- "The usual offices" - bathroom facilities
- "Retired" - went to toilet
- "Unwell" (for women) - menstruating

**Servant/class-specific:**

- "Giving notice" - resigning position
- "In service" - working as domestic servant
- "Daily" - cleaning woman who comes by day
- "Char" (charwoman) - cleaning woman
- "Skivvy" - maid of all work (lowest rank)
- "Below stairs" / "above stairs"
- "Front of house" vs. kitchen staff
- "Upper servant" - butler, housekeeper, lady's maid, valet

**Academic/school slang:**

- "Blue" - athletic honor (Oxford/Cambridge)
- "Scout" - college servant (Oxford)
- "Gyp" - college servant (Cambridge)
- "Rusticated" - temporarily expelled
- "Sent down" - permanently expelled
- "Went up" - began university
- "Come down" - finished/left university
- "Viva" - oral exam
- "Schools" - final exams (Oxford)
- "Tripos" - honors exam (Cambridge)
- "Wrangler" - first-class maths student (Cambridge)
- "Don" - university teacher/fellow
- "Beadle" - university ceremonial officer

**Food/drink expressions:**
